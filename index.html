Project Speculation: The Architecture of Digital Expression

Mediated expression is an act, process, or instance of representing internal states—thoughts, feelings, or experiences—within a specific medium. In this studio, I am exploring how digital tools can transition from being purely utilitarian to becoming instruments for "choice, inflection, and individual adjustment." My speculation centers on the idea of "Technical Expressivity"—the bridge between the rigid logic of professional technical systems and the fluid nuance of creative practice.

Reflections on Experiment One

My first experiment in Information Design explored these themes by redesigning the interface for a web-based synthesizer. Rather than relying on traditional labels like "Frequency" or "Amplitude," I utilized expressive icons to represent "Sonic Temperature" (Ice and Fire) and "Material Density" (Cloud and Brick). This approach leverages "Ambiguity of Information" as a resource for design. By introducing intentional ambiguity, the interface invites the user to interpret and play with the controls, fostering a sense of "craftsmanship and technique" as defined by Murray.

Inspirational Tools and Systems

My conceptual direction is informed by tools that prioritize the *process* of creation over the final output. Townscaper (Oskar Stålberg) is a primary influence; it exemplifies how a tool can support expression by limiting choices to a set of tactile, satisfying interactions. Similarly, the Tiny Tools Directory (Everest Pipkin) showcases the value of specialized, small-scale software that supports specific creative "inflections" rather than general utility.

I have also researched PICO-8 and Hydra as examples of technical systems that encourage expression. PICO-8’s intentional limitations—such as its 128x128 resolution and 16-color palette—demonstrate how creative constraints can actually enhance expressivity. Hydra, on the other hand, allows for real-time visual manipulation through code, treating the browser as a living, expressive medium. These systems prove that even high-level technical logic can be harnessed for individual technique and "reflection-in-action."

Conclusion

Ultimately, my goal for this studio is to develop a tool that values the "individual adjustment" of the user. I believe that by designing for expression rather than just expressive design, we can create digital environments that feel as responsive and nuanced as physical instruments.
